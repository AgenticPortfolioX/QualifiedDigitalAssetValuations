# Charitable Contributions of Cryptocurrency: Why the IRS Requires a Qualified Appraisal Even When Exchange Prices Exist

*Published: April 13, 2026 | By Qualified Digital Asset Valuations (QDAV)*

---

**Cryptocurrency is the only asset class where you can determine the fair market value down to the cent at any moment — and the IRS still requires you to get an independent appraisal before claiming a charitable deduction. This is not a contradiction. It is the law under IRC §170(f)(11), and failing to comply can cost you the entire deduction.**

If you have donated or are considering donating cryptocurrency worth more than $5,000 to a qualified charitable organization, this guide will walk you through the legal requirements, recent enforcement actions, and the specific steps you must take to substantiate your deduction.

## I. The Counterintuitive Rule: Crypto Has a Price, But It Is Not "Readily Valued" Under the Tax Code

The common assumption goes like this: "I donated 2 Bitcoin on March 15. Coinbase shows a closing price of $87,000 that day. My deduction is $174,000. Done."

That assumption is wrong, and the IRS has said so explicitly.

### The IRS Memorandum That Changed Everything

In January 2023, the IRS Office of Chief Counsel released Memorandum 202302012, which addressed a deceptively simple question: **Does cryptocurrency qualify as "publicly traded securities" or other "readily valued property" for purposes of the charitable contribution substantiation rules under IRC §170?**

The answer was unequivocal: **No.**

The memorandum clarified that even though cryptocurrency values can be precisely determined from exchange data, digital assets do not fall within the specific statutory categories of property exempt from the qualified appraisal requirement. Under §170(f)(11)(C), the exemption for "readily valued property" applies only to specific types enumerated in the statute and Treasury Regulations — including cash, publicly traded securities, and certain intellectual property. Cryptocurrency is not among them.

This means that **the substantiation rules for crypto charitable contributions follow the same framework as donations of real estate, fine art, or collectible automobiles.** If the claimed deduction exceeds $5,000, a qualified appraisal is mandatory.

### Why This Distinction Matters

The IRS's position creates a practical reality that many donors find surprising: an asset with continuous price discovery on global exchanges is treated, for charitable deduction purposes, the same as a painting with no market comparables. The rationale is rooted in the statutory text. Congress specified which property types are exempt from appraisal requirements. Digital assets — whether Bitcoin, Ethereum, or any tokenized asset — were not included in the exemption.

For donors, this means:

1. **Exchange screenshots are not sufficient.** You cannot attach a Coinbase statement to your return and call it a day.
2. **The charity's valuation does not count.** The receiving organization is not a "qualified appraiser" under IRS standards, regardless of how it reports the donation.
3. **A qualified appraisal by a qualified appraiser is the only path.** This must meet the timing, content, and credential requirements of §170(f)(11)(C) and the accompanying Treasury Regulations.

## II. The Tiered Substantiation Framework: What the IRS Requires at Every Threshold

The IRS enforces an escalating series of documentation requirements based on the value of the donated property. Understanding where crypto donations fall in this framework is essential.

### Contributions of $250 or More

At this level, the taxpayer must obtain a **contemporaneous written acknowledgment (CWA)** from the charitable organization. The CWA must include:

- The name of the charity
- The date of the contribution
- A description of the property donated (without assigning a value)
- A statement of whether the charity provided any goods or services in exchange for the contribution, and if so, a good-faith estimate of their value

For cryptocurrency donations, the CWA should reference the specific asset (e.g., "2.5 BTC") and the date of transfer. Most reputable charities providing donation receipts for crypto already include this information.

### Contributions Exceeding $500

At the $500 threshold, additional records are required beyond the CWA. The taxpayer must maintain written records that establish:

- **The manner of acquisition** — How the taxpayer obtained the donated property (purchase, mining, airdrop, gift, etc.)
- **The approximate date of acquisition**
- **The cost or adjusted basis** of the property to the donor

This is where crypto donations present unique challenges. If the taxpayer acquired cryptocurrency through a gift, mining, or an airdrop, documenting the basis can require reconstructing historical transactions across multiple wallets, exchanges, and tax years. Meticulous record-keeping at the time of acquisition — not at the time of donation — is critical.

### Contributions Exceeding $5,000

This is the threshold that directly affects most significant crypto donations, and it imposes the most stringent requirements:

1. **A qualified appraisal** must be obtained from a **qualified appraiser.**
2. An **appraisal summary** (Section B of IRS Form 8283) must be completed by the appraiser and attached to the taxpayer's return.
3. The appraisal must be conducted no earlier than **60 days before the date of contribution** and no later than the **due date (including extensions) of the return** on which the deduction is claimed.

### Contributions Exceeding $500,000

For donations of $500,000 or more, all of the above requirements apply, plus:

- **The complete qualified appraisal report must be attached to the tax return** — not merely the summary.

For donors contributing substantial crypto positions — say, an early Bitcoin holder donating 10 BTC at current market prices — this requirement applies directly. The full appraisal document, not just Form 8283 Section B, must accompany the filing.

## III. Gibson v. Commissioner: The Tax Court Speaks on Substantiation Failures

In January 2026, the U.S. Tax Court issued its summary opinion in *Gibson v. Commissioner* (T.C. Summary Opinion 2026-1), a case that crystallizes the consequences of failing to meet the substantiation requirements.

### The Facts

Luke and Breeana Gibson claimed a noncash charitable contribution deduction of $188,563 for donated "high-end cycling EQ apparel." The documentation presented to the IRS was deficient on multiple levels:

- The appraisal was not attached to the original tax return (Form 8283) — it was submitted later during the examination.
- The taxpayer claimed the property was acquired by gift but could not substantiate the donor's cost basis or the acquisition date.
- The taxpayer failed to demonstrate that the appraiser, Eugene H. Ruelle, met the IRS definition of a "qualified appraiser."

### The Court's Ruling

The Tax Court sustained the IRS's full disallowance of the $188,563 deduction. The Court found that the taxpayers failed on every substantive requirement beyond the basic charitable receipt:

- **Basis and acquisition substantiation ($500+ threshold):** The testimony about how the property was obtained was "vague." The Court rejected the argument that obtaining basis information was "impossible," holding that the law "requires detailed substantiation of the basis of the property as well as when the property was acquired."
- **Qualified appraisal requirement ($5,000+ threshold):** The appraisal was submitted late, and the taxpayers could not prove the appraiser was "qualified" under the regulatory definition.

### Why Gibson Matters for Crypto Donors

The *Gibson* decision is a warning shot for cryptocurrency donors. While the case involved physical apparel, the legal framework is identical for digital asset contributions. The key takeaways for crypto donors are:

1. **The burden of proof rests entirely on the taxpayer.** The IRS does not have to prove your deduction is invalid — you have to prove it is valid.
2. **Timing matters.** An appraisal obtained after the IRS begins an examination is not a "qualified appraisal." It must be obtained before the return is filed.
3. **The appraiser's qualifications must be verifiable.** A "qualified appraiser" under §170(f)(11)(E) must meet specific education, experience, and certification requirements. Using an unqualified individual — even if they produce a professional-looking report — will not save the deduction.
4. **Minor documentation failures can be fatal.** The Court did not engage in a "substantial compliance" analysis. Failure to meet the specific statutory requirements resulted in total deduction denial.

For cryptocurrency donors, this means that relying on a hastily assembled exchange screenshot, an after-the-fact valuation by the receiving charity, or an appraisal by someone without digital asset expertise is a strategy that the IRS and the Tax Court have shown they will reject.

## IV. The Practical Filing Process: Form 8283 and Beyond

Filing the correct forms correctly is not optional. Here is what a taxpayer claiming a crypto charitable donation deduction must do.

### Step 1: Obtain the Contemporaneous Written Acknowledgment

After completing the donation, request a written acknowledgment from the charity. This must include:

- The charity's name and EIN
- The date of the contribution
- A description of the donated cryptocurrency (type, quantity, transaction hash if available)
- A statement confirming whether goods or services were provided in exchange

**Important:** Do not rely on a receipt that includes a dollar value. The IRS does not require the charity to state the value, and the charity's stated value has no bearing on the deduction.

### Step 2: Obtain the Qualified Appraisal

Engage a qualified appraiser who meets IRS standards. The appraiser must:

- Hold the necessary education and experience credentials
- Be independent (not the donor, not the charity, not a party to the transaction)
- Conduct the appraisal within the required timeframe (60 days before donation to the return due date)
- Prepare a written appraisal that meets the content requirements of Treas. Reg. §1.170A-17(a)

For cryptocurrency, the appraisal should address:

- The specific digital asset (name, ticker, contract address if applicable)
- The quantity donated
- The valuation date
- The fair market value determination methodology (e.g., average of major exchanges, volume-weighted pricing, time-of-transfer snapshot)
- The appraiser's qualifications and credentials

### Step 3: Complete IRS Form 8283

IRS Form 8283, "Noncash Charitable Contributions," is the required filing document. For donations exceeding $5,000:

- **Section A** (Part I) is completed by the taxpayer — donor information, property description, acquisition details, and claimed value.
- **Section B** (Part IV) is completed by the qualified appraiser — appraiser credentials, valuation, and declaration.
- **Section B** (Part V) is completed by the charity — acknowledgment of receipt and intended use.

The appraiser's signature on Section B is not optional. Without it, the form is incomplete and the deduction is unsupported.

### Step 4: Attach the Appraisal Summary (and Full Appraisal if Required)

For donations of $5,000 to $500,000: attach the completed Form 8283 (including the appraisal summary in Section B) to your tax return.

For donations exceeding $500,000: attach the **complete qualified appraisal report** in addition to Form 8283.

### Step 5: Maintain Records

The IRS can audit returns for three years (or longer in cases of substantial understatement). Maintain all documentation — the CWA, the appraisal, the Form 8283, the charity's acknowledgment, exchange records, and any communications — for at least seven years.

## V. Common Mistakes That Cost Crypto Donors Their Deductions

Based on IRS enforcement patterns, Tax Court decisions, and the specific characteristics of cryptocurrency, here are the most common errors.

### Mistake 1: Relying on Exchange Price Alone

**What donors do:** Attach a screenshot of the exchange price on the date of donation and claim the deduction based on that figure.

**Why it fails:** IRS Memo 202302012 explicitly states that crypto is not "readily valued property" for §170 purposes. An exchange price, while useful for determining fair market value in general tax contexts, does not satisfy the qualified appraisal requirement.

### Mistake 2: Using the Charity's Valuation

**What donors do:** Assume the charity's stated valuation on its donation receipt constitutes a qualified appraisal.

**Why it fails:** The receiving charity is not a "qualified appraiser" under §170(f)(11)(E). The charity is a party to the transaction and therefore disqualified from serving as the appraiser. The IRS has confirmed this position directly.

### Mistake 3: Obtaining the Appraisal After Filing

**What donors do:** File the return claiming the deduction, then obtain the appraisal during an IRS examination.

**Why it fails:** The appraisal must be obtained before the return is filed. An after-the-fact appraisal does not meet the timing requirements of §170(f)(11)(C) and Treas. Reg. §1.170A-13(c)(3). The *Gibson* decision is directly on point.

### Mistake 4: Using an Unqualified Appraiser

**What donors do:** Engage someone who is knowledgeable about cryptocurrency but does not meet the IRS's definition of a "qualified appraiser."

**Why it fails:** A "qualified appraiser" must meet specific credential, education, and independence requirements. Subject-matter expertise alone is not sufficient. The appraiser must also sign the appraisal declaration on Form 8283 Section B under penalties of perjury.

### Mistake 5: Missing the Filing Deadline

**What donors do:** Obtain the appraisal and Form 8283 after the return due date.

**Why it fails:** The appraisal must be obtained no later than the due date of the return, including extensions. Filing an extension (Form 4868) extends this deadline, but failing to file an extension does not.

### Mistake 6: Failing to Document Basis

**What donors do:** Claim the deduction without documenting how they acquired the crypto or what they paid for it.

**Why it fails:** For donations exceeding $500, the taxpayer must substantiate the manner of acquisition, approximate acquisition date, and cost or adjusted basis. The *Gibson* Court specifically noted the taxpayer's failure to establish basis as a grounds for denial.

## VI. What Makes a "Qualified Appraisal" for Cryptocurrency

The Treasury Regulations specify detailed requirements for what constitutes a qualified appraisal. For digital assets, the appraisal must include:

### Content Requirements

1. **A description of the property** — The specific cryptocurrency, including ticker symbol, network, and contract address (for tokens). Simply stating "cryptocurrency" is insufficient.
2. **The date of contribution** — The date the transfer was completed on the blockchain.
3. **The fair market value on the date of contribution** — With the methodology clearly stated.
4. **The method of valuation used** — For crypto, this typically means the average of prices on major exchanges at the time of transfer, or a volume-weighted average price methodology.
5. **The specific basis for the valuation** — Which exchanges were used, what time window was applied, and why that methodology was selected.
6. **The qualifications of the appraiser** — Education, professional credentials, experience with digital asset valuation, and a statement of independence.

### Appraiser Requirements

A qualified appraiser must:

- Have earned an appraisal designation from a recognized professional organization or meet equivalent education and experience requirements
- Regularly perform appraisals for which they receive compensation
- Be independent — not the donor, the donee, or a party to the transaction
- Not be excluded from acting as an appraiser under §170(f)(11)(E)(iii) (i.e., not prohibited from practicing before the IRS)
- Understand the specific characteristics and valuation methodologies applicable to digital assets

For cryptocurrency, the appraiser must also demonstrate competence in digital asset valuation — understanding blockchain transactions, exchange mechanics, token standards, and the specific factors that affect fair market value for different types of digital assets.

## VIII. Frequently Asked Questions

### Q: Can I donate cryptocurrency to any charity?

A: The charity must be a qualified organization under §170(c). Verify the charity's tax-exempt status using the IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search tool before donating. Additionally, confirm that the charity has the infrastructure to receive and process cryptocurrency donations.

### Q: Does the timing of the donation matter?

A: Yes. The donation date for tax purposes is the date the cryptocurrency is irrevocably transferred to the charity's wallet. This is the blockchain confirmation date, not the date you initiated the transfer. The valuation date must match this date.

### Q: What if I donated cryptocurrency multiple times to the same charity?

A: Each donation is a separate charitable contribution. If the aggregate value exceeds $5,000 in a single tax year, a qualified appraisal is required. For multiple donations of the same asset type, the IRS may treat them as "similar items" for aggregation purposes.

### Q: Can I deduct the full fair market value of appreciated cryptocurrency?

A: Generally, yes. For cryptocurrency held for more than one year (long-term capital gain property), you can deduct the full fair market value without recognizing the capital gain. For cryptocurrency held for one year or less, the deduction is limited to your cost basis.

### Q: What if I cannot obtain a qualified appraisal before the filing deadline?

A: File an extension using IRS Form 4868. This extends the deadline for obtaining the appraisal to the extended return due date (typically October 15 for individual filers). Do not file the return claiming the deduction without the appraisal.

### Q: Are there penalties for claiming a deduction without a qualified appraisal?

A: Yes. Beyond the disallowance of the deduction, the IRS may impose accuracy-related penalties under §6662 (typically 20% of the underpayment attributable to the disallowed deduction). For donations exceeding $500,000 without a proper appraisal, a 40% penalty may apply under §6662(h) if the valuation misstatement exceeds certain thresholds.
