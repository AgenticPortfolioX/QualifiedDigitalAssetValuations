# IRS Section 170 and Digital Asset Donations: Why Every Crypto Gift Over $5,000 Requires a Qualified Appraisal

**If you have donated cryptocurrency, NFTs, or any digital asset to a qualified charity and claimed — or plan to claim — a deduction exceeding $5,000, the Internal Revenue Service requires a qualified appraisal. This is not a suggestion. It is a statutory requirement under IRC Section 170(f)(11)(C), and the IRS has confirmed through formal Chief Counsel Advice that the price listed on a cryptocurrency exchange does not satisfy this obligation. Failing to obtain a qualified appraisal will, in most cases, result in the complete denial of your charitable contribution deduction.**

This guide explains exactly what Section 170 requires for digital asset donations, how the IRS treats cryptocurrency compared to other property types, what constitutes a "qualified appraisal" and a "qualified appraiser," and how to navigate the Form 8283 filing process without exposing yourself to audit risk.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Any charitable donation of cryptocurrency or digital assets valued at more than $5,000 requires a qualified appraisal performed by a qualified appraiser.** Exchange-reported prices, wallet balances, and charity-issued receipts are not substitutes.

2. **The IRS formally ruled in CCA 202302012 that cryptocurrency is not a "security" or "readily valued property"** under the Internal Revenue Code. This means crypto does not qualify for the exemptions that apply to publicly traded stocks, bonds, or cash.

3. **You must file IRS Form 8283 (Noncash Charitable Contributions) with your tax return.** For donations exceeding $500,000, the full appraisal report must be attached.

4. **The appraisal must be completed before the due date of your tax return** (including extensions) for the year the contribution was made. Retroactive appraisals do not satisfy the requirement.

5. **The "reasonable cause" exception is extraordinarily difficult to invoke.** Tax Court precedent establishes that multiple references to appraisal requirements on Form 8283 constitute adequate notice. Ignorance of the rule is not a defense.

6. **A USPAP-compliant qualified appraisal is the only defensible standard** for digital asset valuations submitted to the IRS for charitable contribution, estate tax, or Self-Directed IRA purposes.

## I. The Statutory Framework: Section 170 and Noncash Charitable Contributions

### What Section 170 Actually Requires

Section 170 of the Internal Revenue Code governs the deduction of charitable contributions. When a taxpayer donates property — as opposed to cash — to a qualified 501(c)(3) organization, the substantiation and reporting requirements increase significantly with the value of the donated property.

The critical provision is **Section 170(f)(11)(C)**, which states:

> *"No deduction shall be allowed under subsection (a) for any contribution of property for which a deduction of more than $5,000 is claimed unless the taxpayer substantiates the contribution with a qualified appraisal of such property..."*

This language is unambiguous. If you claim a deduction of more than $5,000 for a noncash charitable contribution — including cryptocurrency, NFTs, tokens, or any other digital representation of value — you must obtain a **qualified appraisal**.

### The "Readily Valued Property" Exception (And Why Crypto Does Not Qualify)

The Treasury Regulations under **Section 1.170A-16(d)(2)** provide a narrow list of property types that are exempt from the qualified appraisal requirement because they have "readily ascertainable values." These include:

- Cash
- Certain publicly traded securities (corporate stock listed on a national exchange)
- Inventory held for sale in the ordinary course of business
- Certain vehicles valued using IRS-approved guides

Cryptocurrency does not appear on this list. Despite the fact that Bitcoin, Ethereum, and other tokens trade on public exchanges with published prices, the IRS has determined that digital assets do not meet the statutory definition of a "security" under **Section 165(g)(2)** — which is limited to corporate stock, bonds, debentures, and notes issued by a corporation.

This distinction was formally clarified in **IRS Chief Counsel Advice Memorandum 202302012**, which addressed a taxpayer who donated $10,000 of cryptocurrency to a public charity and attempted to rely on the exchange-reported value without obtaining an appraisal. The IRS's conclusion was unequivocal:

> *"If Taxpayer A donates cryptocurrency for which a charitable contribution deduction of more than $5,000 is claimed, a qualified appraisal of the cryptocurrency is required."*

## II. CCA 202302012: The IRS Puts the Industry on Notice

### What the Chief Counsel Advice Established

In January 2023, the IRS Office of Chief Counsel issued two memoranda addressing cryptocurrency tax treatment. The second of these — **CCA 202302012** — directly addressed the charitable contribution appraisal requirement and established several critical precedents:

**1. Cryptocurrency is property, not currency.** Building on Notice 2014-21, the IRS reaffirmed that digital assets are treated as property for all federal tax purposes. A donation of cryptocurrency is a noncash contribution of property — not a transfer of funds.

**2. Exchange values do not substitute for qualified appraisals.** The IRS specifically rejected the argument that a cryptocurrency's published exchange price satisfies the "readily valued property" exception. The reasoning is definitional: crypto is not a "security" under Section 165(g)(2), and it does not appear on the enumerated list of exempt property types.

**3. The charity's valuation is irrelevant.** Even if the receiving 501(c)(3) organization acknowledges the donation, provides a receipt, or immediately sells the cryptocurrency for a known amount, the charity is not a "qualified appraiser" under IRS standards. The donor must independently obtain a qualified appraisal.

**4. The "reasonable cause" defense has a high bar.** The IRS acknowledged that Section 170(f)(11)(A)(ii)(II) provides a "reasonable cause" exception for taxpayers who fail to obtain an appraisal in good faith. However, the CCA makes clear that this exception is not a blanket forgiveness — it requires proof of ordinary business care and prudence.

### The Tax Court's Position

The CCA cites *Pankratz v. Commissioner*, T.C. Memo. 2021-26, in which the Tax Court addressed a taxpayer's failure to obtain appraisals for noncash donations. The court's observation is instructive:

> *"We think that four mentions of 'appraisal,' 'appraiser,' or 'appraised' on one page of one form is pretty good notice that substantial noncash donations need to be backed up by an appraisal."*

The referenced form was IRS Form 8283 — the same form required for cryptocurrency donations. The court's position is clear: the IRS has provided ample notice of the appraisal requirement, and taxpayers who ignore it do so at their own risk.

## III. What Is a "Qualified Appraisal"?

### The IRS Definition

The term "qualified appraisal" is defined under **Section 170(f)(11)(E)** and further specified in **Regs. Sec. 1.170A-17(a)**. A qualified appraisal must:

1. **Be made no earlier than 60 days before the date of contribution** and no later than the due date (including extensions) of the return on which the deduction is claimed.

2. **Be prepared, signed, and dated by a qualified appraiser** (see Section IV below).

3. **Include the following information:**
   - A detailed description of the donated property
   - The physical condition of the property (if applicable)
   - The date (or expected date) of the contribution
   - The terms of any agreement relating to the use, sale, or other disposition of the donated property
   - The name, address, and taxpayer identification number of the qualified appraiser
   - The qualifications of the appraiser
   - A statement that the appraisal was performed for income tax purposes
   - The date of the appraisal
   - The appraised fair market value on the date of contribution
   - The method of valuation used (market approach, income approach, cost approach)
   - The specific basis for the valuation

4. **Not involve a prohibited fee arrangement.** The appraiser's compensation cannot be based on a percentage of the appraised value or the amount of the deduction claimed.

### Application to Digital Assets

For cryptocurrency and NFT donations, a qualified appraisal must address the unique characteristics of the asset class:

- **For liquid cryptocurrency (e.g., Bitcoin, Ethereum):** The appraiser must document the specific exchange(s) used, the timestamp of the valuation, the volume-weighted average price (if applicable), and any adjustments for transfer restrictions, vesting schedules, or liquidity constraints.

- **For illiquid digital assets (e.g., specific NFTs, governance tokens, locked tokens):** The appraiser must employ a recognized valuation methodology — such as comparable sales analysis, income approach (for yield-bearing tokens), or cost approach — and provide detailed support for the concluded value. A simple reference to a floor price or last sale is insufficient.

- **For complex positions (e.g., LP tokens, staked assets, DeFi positions):** The appraiser must document the underlying mechanics of the position, the associated risks, and the methodology used to derive the fair market value of the donated interest.

## IV. Who Is a "Qualified Appraiser"?

### The Statutory Requirements

Under **Section 170(f)(11)(E)(ii)**, a qualified appraiser must:

1. **Have earned an appraisal designation from a recognized professional appraiser organization** or otherwise meet minimum education and experience requirements set forth in the regulations.

2. **Regularly perform appraisals** for which they receive compensation.

3. **Demonstrate verifiable education and experience** in valuing the type of property being appraised — in this case, digital assets.

4. **Not be excluded from practicing before the IRS** under Section 330(c) of Title 31, United States Code.

5. **Not be the donor, the donee, or a party to the transaction** in which the donor acquired the property.

### The Digital Asset Appraisal Gap

The IRS's definition of "qualified appraiser" creates a specific challenge for digital asset donors: the appraiser must have **verifiable education and experience in valuing the type of property being appraised.**

Currently, no widely recognized professional designation exists specifically for cryptocurrency valuation. Traditional appraiser designations (such as those from the American Society of Appraisers or the Appraisal Institute) focus on real estate, machinery, business interests, and personal property — not digital assets.

This means that a qualified appraiser for cryptocurrency must demonstrate:

- Professional credentials in valuation methodology (e.g., ASA, CVA, ABV designations)
- Specific experience and training in blockchain technology and digital asset markets
- A documented methodology that aligns with USPAP standards
- The ability to produce a report that meets the IRS's requirements under Regs. Sec. 1.170A-17

Firms that combine traditional valuation credentials with deep blockchain expertise are exceptionally rare — which is precisely why donors and their advisors should engage qualified professionals well before the tax filing deadline.

## V. The Form 8283 Filing Process

### Step-by-Step Requirements

**IRS Form 8283 (Noncash Charitable Contributions)** must be filed with your tax return for any noncash charitable contribution exceeding $500. For contributions exceeding $5,000, Section B of the form must be completed, and a qualified appraisal must be obtained.

#### Thresholds and Requirements

| Donation Value | Filing Requirement |
|----------------|-------------------|
| $500 or less | Report on Schedule A (Form 1040); no Form 8283 required |
| $501 – $5,000 | File Form 8283, Section A (no appraisal required) |
| Over $5,000 | File Form 8283, Section B; obtain qualified appraisal |
| Over $500,000 | File Form 8283, Section B; attach full appraisal report to return |

#### Section B: Information Required

When completing Form 8283, Section B for a cryptocurrency donation, you must provide:

1. **Item A:** Description of the donated property (e.g., "12.5 Bitcoin (BTC)")
2. **Item B:** Date of the contribution
3. **Item C:** Date acquired and how acquired (e.g., "Purchased via Coinbase, January 2019")
4. **Item D:** Donor's cost or adjusted basis
5. **Item E:** Fair market value at the time of contribution (as determined by the qualified appraisal)
6. **Item F:** Method used to determine fair market value (e.g., "Market approach using volume-weighted average price")
7. **Appraiser Declaration:** The qualified appraiser must sign a declaration that they are aware of the penalties for overstating value and that the appraisal meets IRS requirements
8. **Donee Acknowledgment:** The charitable organization must sign the form, acknowledging receipt of the donated property

### Critical Deadlines

- The qualified appraisal must be completed **before the due date of the tax return** (including extensions) for the year the contribution was made.
- If you donate cryptocurrency in 2026 and file on extension (October 15, 2027), the appraisal must be completed by October 15, 2027.
- Retroactive appraisals — those completed after the return is filed — do not satisfy the requirement and will result in denial of the deduction.

## VI. What Happens Without a Qualified Appraisal?

### Deduction Denial

If you claim a charitable contribution deduction exceeding $5,000 for cryptocurrency without obtaining a qualified appraisal, the IRS will deny the deduction in full. This is not a penalty or a partial reduction — the entire deduction is disallowed.

### The "Reasonable Cause" Exception

Section 170(f)(11)(A)(ii)(II) provides a narrow exception for taxpayers who can demonstrate "reasonable cause" for failing to obtain an appraisal. To qualify, the taxpayer must show:

1. The failure was due to reasonable cause and not willful neglect.
2. The taxpayer exercised ordinary business care and prudence.
3. The taxpayer relied on professional tax advice (not self-prepared returns).

The Tax Court has consistently held this exception to a high standard. In *Pankratz*, the court noted that Form 8283 itself provides multiple warnings about the appraisal requirement — making it difficult for any taxpayer to claim they were unaware of the obligation.

### Additional Consequences

Beyond deduction denial, failure to obtain a qualified appraisal can trigger:

- **Accuracy-related penalties** under Section 6662 (typically 20% of the underpayment attributable to the disallowed deduction)
- **Increased audit scrutiny** of the taxpayer's broader return
- **Statute of limitations complications** — if the deduction is denied, the resulting tax deficiency extends the assessment period

## VII. Strategic Considerations for Donors

### Timing Your Donation

The qualified appraisal requirement introduces a timing element that donors must plan for:

1. **Engage an appraiser before the donation.** While the appraisal cannot be completed until the contribution date is known, the appraiser should be identified and engaged in advance.

2. **Document the donation contemporaneously.** At the time of transfer, record the blockchain transaction hash, the exact timestamp, the wallet addresses involved, and the exchange rate at that moment.

3. **Secure the donee acknowledgment promptly.** The charitable organization must sign Form 8283, Section B. Organizations that are unfamiliar with digital asset donations may require additional education.

4. **Coordinate with your tax advisor.** The appraisal, Form 8283, and the tax return must align. Your CPA or tax attorney should review the appraisal before filing.

### The Tax Efficiency of Donating Appreciated Digital Assets

Donating cryptocurrency that has been held for more than one year (long-term capital gain property) provides significant tax advantages over selling the asset and donating the proceeds:

- **Avoid capital gains tax.** You do not pay capital gains on the appreciation when you donate the asset directly.
- **Deduct the full fair market value.** You may deduct the full FMV of the donated asset — not just your cost basis — subject to AGI limitations (generally 30% of AGF for contributions to public charities).
- **Double tax benefit.** You receive both a deduction for the FMV and avoid the capital gains tax that would have been due on sale.

However, these benefits are **entirely contingent on obtaining a qualified appraisal.** Without it, the deduction — and the entire tax strategy — collapses.

## VIII. Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need a qualified appraisal for donating Bitcoin?

**Yes, if the claimed deduction exceeds $5,000.** Despite Bitcoin trading on public exchanges, the IRS has determined that cryptocurrency does not qualify as a "publicly traded security" or "readily valued property" exempt from the appraisal requirement. A qualified appraisal is mandatory under Section 170(f)(11)(C).

### Can I use the exchange price as my valuation?

**No.** The IRS Chief Counsel Advice Memorandum 202302012 specifically addresses this question. Exchange-reported values do not constitute a qualified appraisal, and relying on them as a substitute will result in deduction denial.

### Does the charity need to get the appraisal?

**No.** The appraisal obligation falls on the donor, not the donee. The charity must sign Form 8283 to acknowledge receipt of the donation, but it is not responsible for obtaining or providing the valuation. The charity's own valuation or acknowledgment does not satisfy the qualified appraisal requirement.

### What if I donate less than $5,000 worth of cryptocurrency?

If your total claimed deduction for all noncash charitable contributions in a tax year is $500 or less, you report the donation on Schedule A without Form 8283. Between $501 and $5,000, you must file Form 8283, Section A, but a qualified appraisal is not required. The qualified appraisal requirement applies only when the claimed deduction exceeds $5,000.

### What makes an appraiser "qualified" for digital assets?

A qualified appraiser for digital assets must hold professional valuation credentials, demonstrate specific education and experience in valuing the type of digital asset being donated, and produce an appraisal that meets USPAP standards and IRS requirements under Regs. Sec. 1.170A-17. The appraiser cannot be the donor, the donee, or a party to the original acquisition.

### How long does the appraisal process take?

The timeline depends on the complexity of the donated asset. A liquid cryptocurrency (e.g., Bitcoin) donation may be completed within days, while complex NFT collections or DeFi positions may require additional analysis. Donors should engage an appraiser at least 30 days before their tax filing deadline to ensure timely completion.

### Can I deduct an NFT donation?

**Yes, subject to the same rules as any other noncash charitable contribution.** NFT donations exceeding $5,000 require a qualified appraisal. The appraisal must address the specific characteristics of the NFT — including rarity traits, provenance, creator reputation, comparable sales, and market conditions — to support the concluded fair market value.
