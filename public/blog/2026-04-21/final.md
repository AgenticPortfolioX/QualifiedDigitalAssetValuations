# Crypto Estate Tax Valuation: Step-Up Basis, Form 706, and the Qualified Appraisal Requirement for Digital Assets

*Published: April 21, 2026 | By Qualified Digital Asset Valuations (QDAV)*

---

**When an individual passes away holding cryptocurrency, the estate faces a dual obligation that few practitioners fully understand: determining the fair market value of digital assets for estate tax purposes on Form 706, and establishing a defensible step-up in basis for beneficiaries under IRC §1014. Both require professional-grade valuation methodology — and for significant holdings, both trigger the IRS's qualified appraisal requirement.**

The intersection of estate tax law and digital asset valuation is one of the most underserved areas in contemporary tax practice. As cryptocurrency holdings among high-net-worth individuals continue to grow, estate attorneys and CPAs are confronting valuation challenges that traditional frameworks were never designed to address.

This guide provides a comprehensive analysis of the IRS requirements, valuation methodologies, and compliance obligations that govern digital asset estate tax reporting.

---

## I. The Estate Tax Framework for Digital Assets

### A. Gross Estate Inclusion Under IRC §2031

Under IRC §2031, the gross estate includes all property owned at the date of death, valued at fair market value (FMV). This provision applies without exception to cryptocurrency, NFTs, DeFi positions, and all other digital assets. The IRS does not distinguish between Bitcoin held on a centralized exchange and an NFT stored in a self-custodied wallet — both must be reported on Form 706 (United States Estate and Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax Return).

The FMV of digital assets is determined as of either:

1. **The date of the decedent's death**, or
2. **The alternate valuation date** (six months after death), if elected under IRC §2032

The alternate valuation election is available only if it reduces both the gross estate value and the estate tax liability. For volatile asset classes like cryptocurrency, this election can have significant implications. A Bitcoin position worth $800,000 on the date of death might be worth $620,000 six months later — or $1,100,000. The executor must evaluate whether the alternate valuation date produces a net benefit.

### B. What Qualifies as a Digital Asset for Estate Tax Purposes

The IRS defines "digital assets" broadly. For estate tax reporting, the following are included:

- **Cryptocurrency:** Bitcoin (BTC), Ethereum (ETH), and all altcoins
- **Stablecoins:** USDT, USDC, DAI, and algorithmic stablecoins
- **Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs):** Unique digital collectibles, art, and utility tokens
- **DeFi Positions:** Liquidity provider (LP) tokens, staking positions, yield farming rewards
- **Wrapped Tokens:** WBTC, stETH, and other tokenized representations
- **Airdropped Tokens:** Tokens received post-mortem may require separate analysis
- **Governance Tokens:** Tokens conferring voting rights in decentralized protocols

Each category presents distinct valuation challenges, which we address in detail below.

## II. The Step-Up in Basis: IRC §1014 and Inherited Cryptocurrency

### A. Mechanics of the Step-Up

Under IRC §1014(a), the basis of property acquired from a decedent is generally the FMV of the property at the date of the decedent's death. This "step-up in basis" eliminates unrealized capital gains that accrued during the decedent's lifetime.

**Example:** A decedent purchased 10 Bitcoin in 2017 for $10,000 total ($1,000 per BTC). At the date of death in 2026, Bitcoin trades at $87,000 per BTC, making the position worth $870,000. The beneficiary receives a stepped-up basis of $870,000. If the beneficiary later sells at $90,000 per BTC ($900,000 total), the taxable gain is only $30,000 — not $890,000.

This is one of the most significant tax benefits available to cryptocurrency holders, and it underscores why accurate date-of-death valuation is critical.

### B. Alternate Valuation and Volatility

If the executor elects alternate valuation under IRC §2032, the basis of inherited property is the FMV on the date six months after death — but only if the property is still held by the estate or distributed to a beneficiary without being sold.

For cryptocurrency, the extreme volatility creates complex scenarios:

- **If crypto appreciates** between date of death and the alternate date, the beneficiary receives a higher stepped-up basis but the estate faces a higher gross estate value
- **If crypto depreciates**, the estate tax decreases but the beneficiary's stepped-up basis also decreases
- **If crypto is sold** between the two dates, the sale price controls

The executor must model these scenarios carefully, particularly for large positions.

### C. Carried-Over Basis Exceptions

The step-up does not apply in all situations:

- **Gifts with retained interests (IRC §1014(e)):** Property that was gifted within one year of death and returned to the decedent does not receive a step-up
- **Income in respect of a decedent (IRD) under IRC §691:** Certain DeFi staking rewards and income that accrued but was not received before death may be treated as IRD and not eligible for the step-up
- **Property acquired by gift within 3 years of death (special rules for certain transfers)**

## III. Form 706 Reporting Requirements for Digital Assets

### A. Schedule B and Schedule E

Form 706 requires detailed reporting of estate assets:

- **Schedule B:** Reports stocks, bonds, and other financial instruments. Cryptocurrency held through brokerage accounts or exchange-traded funds may be reported here.
- **Schedule E:** Reports joint interests in property. If digital assets are held jointly, the surviving joint tenant's interest must be documented.
- **Schedule F:** Reports other miscellaneous property, including cryptocurrency held directly in wallets, NFTs, and DeFi positions.

### B. Valuation Documentation Requirements

The IRS requires that each asset be supported by adequate documentation. For digital assets, this includes:

1. **Wallet addresses** and corresponding blockchain explorers (e.g., Etherscan, Blockchain.com)
2. **Exchange account statements** showing holdings as of the date of death
3. **Transaction history** demonstrating the decedent's acquisition dates and cost basis
4. **Valuation methodology** applied, particularly for illiquid or unique assets
5. **Qualified appraisal** (when required — see Section IV)

### C. Common Form 706 Errors with Digital Assets

Based on IRS enforcement patterns, the following errors frequently trigger audit attention:

- **Failure to report all wallets:** Many crypto holders maintain multiple wallets across multiple chains. Estate representatives may discover wallets months or years after the filing deadline.
- **Incorrect FMV determination:** Using a single exchange's price without considering liquidity, spread, or market depth.
- **Missing NFTs:** NFTs in self-custodied wallets are easily overlooked and often represent significant value.
- **DeFi position neglect:** LP tokens, staking positions, and yield farming rewards are routinely omitted from estate filings.
- **No qualified appraisal:** For high-value holdings, the failure to obtain a USPAP-compliant appraisal creates a deficiency that can result in substantial penalties.

## IV. The Qualified Appraisal Requirement for Estate Valuations

### A. When Is a Qualified Appraisal Required?

The qualified appraisal requirement applies to estate tax purposes in specific circumstances:

1. **IRS Audit Defense:** While the IRC §170 charitable contribution rules explicitly mandate a qualified appraisal for donations exceeding $5,000, the estate tax context places a strong emphasis on defensible valuations. IRS examiners routinely request appraisal documentation for digital asset positions exceeding $10,000.
2. **Illiquid Asset Valuation:** NFTs, governance tokens with limited trading history, and DeFi positions require professional valuation methodologies that go beyond simple price lookup.
3. **Penalty Mitigation:** A USPAP-compliant qualified appraisal provides the "reasonable cause" defense against accuracy-related penalties under IRC §6662.

### B. USPAP Standards Applied to Digital Assets

The Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice (USPAP) establish the framework for qualified appraisals. When applied to digital assets, USPAP requires:

- **Scope of Work:** Clearly defined parameters of the valuation engagement
- **Market Analysis:** Assessment of relevant markets, including exchange-based and OTC markets
- **Valuation Approaches:** At minimum, the Market Approach (comparing to similar transactions) and consideration of the Income Approach (for DeFi yield-generating positions)
- **Highest and Best Use:** Analysis of the asset's most productive use
- **Effective Date:** Precise date-of-death or alternate valuation date determination
- **Certification:** Appraiser's certification of independence, competency, and compliance

### C. Who Can Perform a Qualified Appraisal?

The IRS requires that qualified appraisals be performed by:

- A **qualified appraiser** as defined under IRC §170(f)(11)(E)(ii)
- An individual with **relevant education, experience, and professional credentials**
- A professional who is **independent** of the taxpayer and the transaction
- A practitioner who complies with **USPAP standards**

For digital assets, this means engaging a professional who understands both valuation methodology and the technical aspects of blockchain-based assets. A traditional real estate appraiser or fine art valuator, however credentialed, may lack the specialized knowledge required for cryptocurrency and NFT valuations.

## V. Valuation Methodologies by Digital Asset Category

### A. Liquid Cryptocurrency (Bitcoin, Ethereum, Major Altcoins)

For liquid cryptocurrencies traded on major exchanges, the Market Approach is straightforward:

1. Identify the FMV on the date of death using volume-weighted average price (VWAP) across major exchanges
2. Adjust for any transfer restrictions or lock-up periods
3. Document the source and methodology of price determination
4. Consider market depth — a $5 million position in a low-cap altcoin cannot be liquidated at the quoted spot price without significant slippage

**Key Consideration:** The IRS has not issued specific guidance on which exchange or pricing methodology to use. However, professional practice favors VWAP from the top exchanges by volume, supplemented by corroborating sources.

### B. Non-Fungible Tokens (NFTs)

NFT valuation presents unique challenges:

1. **Comparable Sales:** Identify sales of NFTs from the same collection with similar traits
2. **Trait Rarity Analysis:** Use rarity ranking tools to position the specific NFT within its collection
3. **Collection Floor Price:** Establish the minimum value based on collection floor
4. **Creator/Artist Premium:** For art NFTs, the creator's reputation and sales history affect value
5. **Utility Value:** NFTs with access passes, gaming utility, or token-gated benefits require separate analysis

**Example:** A Bored Ape Yacht Club NFT with "laser eyes" and "gold fur" traits would be valued based on the rarity of those traits, recent sales of comparable apes, the collection's floor price at the date of death, and any associated benefits (e.g., mutant ape airdrops, APE token allocations).

### C. DeFi Positions

DeFi protocols introduce valuation complexities:

1. **Liquidity Provider (LP) Tokens:** Valued based on the underlying assets in the liquidity pool at the date of death
2. **Staking Positions:** Valued at the underlying token value plus accrued but undistributed rewards
3. **Yield Farming Rewards:** Treated as income in respect of a decedent (IRD) if accrued but not claimed before death
4. **Lending Positions:** Collateral and borrowed amounts must be netted to determine the estate's economic interest
5. **Governance Tokens:** May carry premium value if the DAO treasury is substantial

### D. Wrapped and Bridged Tokens

Wrapped tokens (WBTC, stETH) and assets on Layer 2 networks or bridges require:

1. Verification that the underlying asset backing is intact as of the date of death
2. Assessment of any depeg risk that was present at the valuation date
3. Consideration of bridge security and counterparty risk

## VI. Estate Planning Strategies for Digital Asset Holders

### A. Pre-Mortem Documentation

The single most impactful action a digital asset holder can take is documenting their holdings:

1. **Comprehensive inventory** of all wallets, exchanges, and DeFi positions
2. **Access credentials** stored in a secure, discoverable location (estate planning attorney vault, hardware wallet recovery seed in a safe)
3. **Cost basis records** for all acquisitions
4. **Valuation history** — periodic professional appraisals create a defensible baseline

### B. Trust Structures

Digital assets can be held in various trust structures:

- **Revocable Living Trusts:** Avoid probate while maintaining control; basis step-up applies at death
- **Irrevocable Life Insurance Trusts (ILITs):** Can be funded with digital assets to remove them from the taxable estate
- **Grantor Retained Annuity Trusts (GRATs):** Effective for appreciating digital assets, transferring future appreciation to beneficiaries with minimal gift tax
- **Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRTs):** Provide income tax deduction and income stream while removing digital assets from the estate

### C. Gifting Strategies

- **Annual Exclusion:** Currently $18,000 per recipient (2026) — can gift cryptocurrency within this limit without gift tax consequences
- **Lifetime Exclusion:** The unified credit (currently approximately $13.61 million per individual) can shelter significant digital asset transfers
- **Basis Consideration:** Gifted property carries over the donor's basis (no step-up) — strategic gifting should account for this

## VII. Audit Defense: Protecting the Estate Valuation

### A. Documentation as Defense

The strongest defense against IRS challenge is comprehensive documentation:

- Qualified appraisal with USPAP compliance
- Blockchain transaction records corroborating the valuation
- Exchange records and price data from multiple sources
- Expert methodology explanation accessible to non-technical reviewers

### B. Penalty Exposure

Without a qualified appraisal, estates face:

- **IRC §6662 Accuracy-Related Penalty:** 20% of the underpayment attributable to negligence or substantial understatement
- **IRC §6662(e) Valuation Misstatement:** 20% penalty if the reported value is 65% or less of the correct value; 40% if 40% or less
- **IRC §6663 Fraud Penalty:** 75% of the underpayment in cases of fraud

### C. Statute of Limitations

The IRS generally has three years from the date of filing Form 706 to assess additional estate tax. However:

- **Fraud:** No statute of limitations
- **Substantial Understatement (65% rule):** Six-year statute
- **Failure to file:** No statute of limitations

## Frequently Asked Questions

**When is a qualified appraisal needed for inherited cryptocurrency?**
While the explicit qualified appraisal mandate under IRC §170 applies to charitable contributions, the IRS strongly expects USPAP-compliant valuations for estate tax purposes — particularly for digital asset positions exceeding $10,000, illiquid assets like NFTs, or any holding that may face audit scrutiny. A qualified appraisal provides the "reasonable cause" defense against accuracy-related penalties under IRC §6662.

**How is fair market value determined for inherited digital assets?**
Fair market value is determined as of the date of death (or alternate valuation date, if elected) using the Market Approach — typically volume-weighted average price (VWAP) from major exchanges for liquid cryptocurrency. For illiquid assets like NFTs and DeFi positions, comparable sales analysis, trait rarity assessment, and underlying asset valuation are required.

**What is the step-up in basis for cryptocurrency?**
Under IRC §1014, the beneficiary's cost basis in inherited cryptocurrency is "stepped up" to the fair market value at the date of the decedent's death. This eliminates unrealized capital gains accrued during the decedent's lifetime. For example, Bitcoin purchased at $1,000 and inherited at $87,000 receives a stepped-up basis of $87,000.

**How do you report cryptocurrency on IRS Form 706?**
Digital assets are reported on Form 706 using Schedule F (Other Miscellaneous Property) for directly held cryptocurrency, NFTs, and DeFi positions. Each asset must be listed with its fair market value, supported by exchange records, blockchain transaction history, and valuation methodology documentation.

**What makes a digital asset valuation "qualified" under IRS rules?**
A qualified appraisal must be performed by a qualified appraiser, comply with USPAP standards, include a detailed description of the asset, the valuation date, the valuation methodology, the appraiser's qualifications, and a certification of independence and compliance. The appraiser must have relevant education, experience, and professional credentials specific to digital asset valuation.
